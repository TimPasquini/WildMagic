# Wild Magic — World & Realms

Drafted 2026-06. Companion to `AESTHETICS_AND_TONE.md` (the voice/look bible) and the
emergent-world docs. The tone bible governs *how* this world is rendered; this document
is *what* the world is. Where they touch, the tone bible wins on register and this one
wins on fact.

The frame to keep in mind while reading: **COLOR vs MARBLE.** Everything below is the
color. The Empire is the marble — genuinely beautiful, genuinely orderly, genuinely not
evil, and it is slowly pressing every realm here flat into permits and ledgers. You are
the pest it has not yet filed.

**Geography is procedural.** The map is freshly generated each run; what's fixed is
*character*, not coordinates. Two hard rules: the three conquered old kingdoms are always
adjacent to Vigovia, and exactly one of the four old kingdoms is, each run, the unconquered
**rival** — the last realm still openly allowing wild magic and the Empire's standing
military headache. Which one rotates run to run, so each is written to play both parts.

---

## How magic actually works (the one unifying truth)

Magic in this world is **inherently wild.** There is one raw, living substrate, and it
does not love you back. The famous "traditions" — crystal, bone, blood, woven, ink-and-song,
curse, horse-song, the yoghurt rites, and charter magic — are **not different magics.** They are *cultural
dialects*: different peoples' ways of reaching the same wild source, dressed in different
gestures, materials, and manners. In principle any tradition can do anything any other can.
The bone-singer and the crystal-keyer are reaching into the same fire; they only hold it
differently, and call it by different names.

Two consequences worth holding onto:

- **The traditions are geography, not just history** (per the tone bible). The Parn really
  do sing magic into ink today; the merfolk keep their own rites now, not as folklore. A
  tradition is a living accent, and the dungeon's buried strata are the accents that didn't
  survive.
- **Crystals are the a tradition with a genuine material edge.** A crystal *stores*
  magical energy: one person can pour power into it slowly and another can draw and channel
  it later, easily. That property is real, not cultural — which is why crystal magic spread
  and why Stalnaz built a civilization on it. Everything else crystal magic "does" is the
  usual wild substrate in Stalnazan dress.

**Almost everyone has a little capacity.** Most people, if they truly reached for it, could
work a flicker of wild magic; raw strength varies enormously from person to person. The
player is an *unusually* strong sorcerer — an outlier, not a chosen one. This is why the
prohibition is a live social tension rather than an abstraction: the thing being outlawed
is latent in nearly every citizen.

**Charter magic** is the Empire's answer: the wild substrate, licensed. Charter mages train
for years to perform a fixed, approved repertoire of spells with cold, repeatable precision —
the dangerous parts filed off, the rest stamped and permitted. The charter didn't invent new
magic; it *subsumed* the old traditions, allowing certain versions of certain spells and
outlawing anything it deems dangerous. A modern woven charm, a chartered yoghurt rite, a
blessed Parn tattoo — all are old-tradition forms now performed by a licensed charter mage
instead of a folk practitioner. (See the tone bible on why people genuinely *choose* it.)

## The Grand Empire of Vigovia

Founded long ago as a **kingdom** by **Vigo the Lawgiver**, now grown into an empire that
has fully conquered three other realms. In common speech the names split by scale:
**"Vigovia"** means the original kingdom — the heartland, Vigo's own country — while the whole
construction is just **"the Empire"** or **"the Grand Empire."** Calling the whole empire
"Vigovia" would be often mistaken as meaning the original country.

### The heartland and its people

Vigovian culture is **stodgy, orderly, vaguely Prussian** — earnest, proper, a little
humorless, deeply proud of its institutions. The men wear **full bushy mustaches.** It is a
**bureaucratic** society to its bones: much of life runs on permits, approvals, filings, and
schedules, and so **lawyers are highly paid and highly respected.** To become a **charter
mage** is a great honor — the social weight of becoming a doctor — a clean, prestigious,
licensed profession, the respectable opposite of the feral sorcerer. The Vigovians keep a
**pantheon** (shared with Ryolan and Monteary; see below).

The Empire's menace, per the tone bible, is never gothic — it is *reasonable*. Vigovians
genuinely believe the charter saved the world, and they are not entirely wrong.

### The emperor

The emperor is the living head of the **Vigovian dynasty**, Vigo's line. He signs at the top 
of a chain of warrants that ends, far below, with the field squadrons that close your file.

### The Shadow Purge — the justification for the charter

The haunting horror, and the Empire's perpetual justification. A cult of **shadow sorcerers**
sacrificed their own lives in a great working that loosed **shadow spirits** through a major
Vigovian city. The spirits **leapt from body to body**, possessing ordinary people and
driving them to murder those nearest them — family, friends, neighbors. The **Shadow Purge**
is the catastrophe every Vigovian schoolchild knows.

The crucial, un-cartoonish part: the Purge was *partly a reaction* to Vigovia's already-rising
push to charterize magic — wild mages striking back at the coming order — and the Empire then
used it as the moral engine for a far harsher crackdown. But Vigovia's leaders **already**
believed wild magic too dangerous to leave free, **and** they had already seen the cold
strategic prize: a corps of highly trained, highly controlled charter mages who answer **only
to them.** The Purge didn't create the policy. It sanctified it. That doubled motive — real
horror plus real ambition — is exactly the moral texture the tone bible wants: the Empire is
sincere *and* self-serving, and untangling the two is the player's whole problem.

The Empire's enforcement arm is the **Censorate** (see tone bible): the office that issues
Thaumic Containment Orders, posts bounties, and files the incident reports you find — fronted
in the field by faceless numbered squadrons and, on paper, by one weary recurring clerk.

### The shape of the conquest

The Empire has fully conquered **three** realms; one realm remains the **rival.** Each run,
three of the four **old kingdoms** (Stalnaz, Brall, Ryolan, Vint) are conquered and adjacent
to the heartland, and the fourth is the free rival. Along with them sits the nominally independent
client kingdom of **Threen**, and a number of **small realms** that are not conquered but know
better than to push back — they officially ban wild magic and let imperial soldiers cross
their land.

**Separatists** exist in every conquered realm, but they are usually **quiet** — embers, not
fires. The one exception is **Vint**, where the separatist cause is loud and legitimate: an
open, vocal **political party.**

## The Four Old Kingdoms

Each is written to play **both** roles. *When free (the rival)* gives its defiant flavor —
how it fights with wild magic unchained. *When conquered* gives its honest reasons for
making peace, the thing that lets a proud people credibly choose the marble.

### Stalnaz — the Queendom of crystal and song

Renowned across the world for **fine music, art, and philosophy** — the realm everyone else
secretly measures their own refinement against. Its signature dialect is **crystal magic**,
built on the one tradition with a real material edge: crystals that **store** magic so one
hand may charge what another later channels. Stalnaz keeps **its own pantheon.**

**A queendom by hard-won design.** Once an ordinary kingdom, Stalnaz suffered a succession of
**terrible kings** that nearly ruined it. The **wife of the last king murdered him**, took the
throne, and restored the realm to glory. Ever since, Stalnaz is ruled by a **queen**, and the
founding regicide is no buried shame — it's **openly celebrated**, with a great annual
**holiday** in the first queen's honor.

**Succession by chosen heiress.** Each queen names her own successor. Often a **daughter** —
but there is a strong, healthy tradition of choosing a worthy, **unrelated woman** and
grooming her as protégée. The institution is so settled it rarely breeds open tension:
**blood-daughters are always well cared for and are more influential than sons**, and it
is drilled into every royal child that *not all are fit to rule* — the wicked kings stand as
the eternal cautionary tale. A queen typically keeps a few candidates quietly in mind and, in
time, **summons one to court** as protégée. The **current queen has no children**; she is
believed to have her eye on several women, but **none has yet been summoned** — a slow,
watched, realm-wide suspense.

- *When free (the rival):* a defiant queendom that simply **refuses to charterize**, keeping
  crystal-song open and its arsenals of pre-charged crystals as both art and armory; fights
  as much with cultural prestige and patient soft power as with force.
- *When conquered:* a realm that has survived terrible rulers before and prizes **continuity
  over ruin**; the charter lets it keep chartered crystal-music and its court intact, and a
  pragmatic queen judges the realm's genius to be cultural, not martial — better to endure and
  outlast than burn.

### Brall — the holds of bone and ale

A boisterous people who love **ale, tall tales, and songs.** Their signature art is
**scrimshaw**; their magical dialect is **bone magic.** They are ruled by the **Bone Jarls** —
the lords of the individual **holds**, who together form a council. Their religion is a body
of **legendary heroes**, almost certainly real people whose deeds have been bolstered into myth.

**The bones** come from **whales, beasts, and men** — though working **human bone is strictly
taboo** under the charter, a bright line the Empire does not bend.

**A pride quietly wounded.** Before the conquest the Bone Jarls did their **own** bone magic.
Now their workings are performed by **charter mages** on their behalf. The common folk
sometimes feel it as a genuine insult — to be governed by Bone Jarls who no longer sing their
own bone — but Bralli are **superbly good at distracting themselves** with drink and stories,
and the wound mostly stays drowned.

**The tall-tale art** is not boasting. Bralli rarely brag about *themselves*; the craft is
telling tales about **others** — what you saw, what your uncle did, what an ancestor once did —
and the joy is **collaborative escalation**: each teller builds the last tale wilder, everyone
**pretending to believe**, all in good sport. It's the warmth of the culture in miniature, and
it bleeds into how the Jarls' council actually talks.

- *When free (the rival):* the Jarls sing their own bone again; whale-bone war-craft, skalds,
  and hold-levies make Brall a blunt, defiant martial thorn.
- *When conquered:* a people who'd rather keep the ale flowing and the hall warm than bleed for
  pride; the Jarls keep their seats and their council, charter mages do the working, and the
  tall tales paper over the rest.

### Ryolan — the kingdom of honor and blood

A land of **honor and the duel.** Ryolani love **chariot races** and follow their teams with
real passion — fights break out over them. Their dialect is **blood magic**, and they are a
**monarchy** under a **king.** They share the **Vigovian pantheon**.

**Blood magic and the duel are kin, not the same.** It is custom to open a duel by cutting
your own **off-hand palm** — a gesture *descended* from blood magic, though today it is just
honored tradition. Actual wild blood magic could, like any wild magic, manifest in nearly any
way; but to cast it before the crowd that gathers at a Ryolani duel, you'd have to be
extraordinarily brave or extraordinarily foolish. The honor/shame culture is the water Ryolani
swim in: oaths bind, face matters, and the duel is the pressure valve.

**The chariot teams** are not castes or bloodlines — allegiance is **personal.** Some pick by
**geography**, some purely for a team's **style**, some switch for a spouse or a friend.
Rooting for **more than one** team is allowed but will get you heckled. It's fandom, fierce
and chosen, not destiny.

- *When free (the rival):* the most openly **militant** holdout — a martial honor-culture of
  blood-duelists and a king's host, the realm that meets the Empire blade-first.
- *When conquered:* honor cuts toward peace too. **An oath given is an oath kept**; a king who
  judged the war unwinnable and swore terms is bound by his own code to honor them, and a
  proud people can find dignity in keeping faith rather than in a hopeless fight.

### Vint — the woven republic

Famed for **gossip and intrigue.** Their dialect is **woven magic** — charms embroidered into
**tapestries, clothes, scarves**, and the like. They are a **republic**, and politics is the
national sport, all the sweeter because it usually rides on especially **juicy gossip.** Vint
is the one realm that **doesn't much care for religion.**

**Woven charms are everywhere** in Vint — a healing scarf, an anti-eavesdropping shawl, a
child's protective swaddle — though these days the charms are **bought from charter mages.**
Magic **tapestries** are common and a famous **export**: you'll see expensive Vintan tapestries
hung in halls across the world, though Vintan **woven charms themselves are rarely worn by
non-Vintans**. The cloth carries the culture abroad even where the magic stays home.

**The politics is gloriously unstable.** It is driven far more by **rumor and gossip** than by
policy, so the **politicians** churn constantly — upheaval of the ruling figures is more common
than not. But here's the trick: **policy changes far less than politics.** The officeholders
mostly leave the actual machinery in place, so the *common people* live a steadier life than
the frantic political class. Politicians are **celebrities**, on every tongue, which is exactly
why so many crave the life despite its short tenure.

- *When free (the rival):* Vint fights with **whispers** — intrigue, woven spy-craft, rumor as
  a weapon, and quiet money funneled to everyone else's resistance.
- *When conquered:* the merchants and the assembly prefer **stable policy and open trade** to
  ruin, and gossip thrives under any flag. But Vint is the **one** conquered realm where the
  separatist cause is a **loud, legitimate political party**, not a hidden ember.

## Threen — the Independent Kingdom

Officially independent; **everyone knows it is de facto ruled by the emperor.** Its capital is
a beautiful **city of many canals**. Threen is **fairly wealthy**,
takes deep pride in its **literature**, and supports a strong culture of **artisans** who
produce **finished goods for export.**

The polite fiction is maintained with care on both sides. Threen **rarely lets imperial
soldiers patrol its streets** — that would break the spell — but **imperial emissaries** are a
common sight, and they **play along**, treating the Threenians with genuine respect. Underneath
the courtesy, both sides know the truth plainly: Threen has **nowhere near** the military to
resist if the Empire chose to take it, and so it **doesn't even try.** Independence here is a
shared performance that everyone finds more comfortable than the alternative.

## The Smaller Realms & Peoples

Not conquered, but living in the Empire's gravity: they **officially ban wild magic** and
**let imperial soldiers cross their lands.** Heavily influenced, carefully deferential.

### Monteary — the horse-realm

One main **city** and its surrounds, famous across the world for **one thing: horses.** Monteary
breeds **by far the best horses anywhere**, prized above all for its **geldings.** A common
phrase is **"No one walks out of Monteary"** since, more likely than not, they came to buy a horse.
It guards the breed jealously — selling only **geldings and mares**, with **stallions strictly controlled** 
to protect the monopoly. They share the **Vigovian pantheon.**

The open secret is **horse-singing**: some Montearians still *talk to* their horses, using magic
to make themselves understood — never within an imperial's earshot. It's rarely done anywhere
public enough to be caught, and for good reason: a horse-singer **caught** working magic would be
**executed as a sorcerer**, like any wild mage.

### Ontria — the yoghurt tribes

A land of **tribes**, each with its **own living culture of yoghurt** — communally kept,
ritually consumed, and woven into the heart of the clan. Eating a clan's yoghurt grants
**superhuman powers**, and because **each clan's culture is different, the powers differ** —
which gives every clan its **distinct character.** Ontrians are deep **ancestor-worshippers**,
and many do not speak Common.

The rite: **respected men ritually spit into the yoghurt**, overseen by **shamans** working
magic rites. Spitting is how a person **passes their own personal strengths into the clan** —
and since the culture is kept alive forever, never allowed to die, it is understood as passing
your **legacy to posterity**, a perfect fit with their ancestor worship. The **main rituals are
now chartered**, so the practice is permitted and the **shamans are now charter mages** — one of
the cleaner examples of the charter absorbing a living tradition rather than crushing it.

### Gontark — the goatfolk

**Literally caprine** — a people of horns and goat's eyes — known for their **vicious curses.**
The curses are real wild magic, and so genuinely **feared**: unpredictable, dangerous, not to be
provoked. They keep **their own gods**, and many do not speak Common. To **curse for pay** is
viewed roughly the way the rest of the world views a **hired killer** — illegal, underground,
disreputable — not a service a respectable goatfolk would advertise.

### The Parn — the desert caravans

A **nomadic desert people**, heavily **tattooed** and devoted to **music** (the world's living
**sound-magic** dialect, per the tone bible). They call themselves the **Parn.** Their magic
lives sometimes **in the ink** — though these days a Parn tattoo is as often **done or blessed by
a charter mage**, and sometimes it's simply a mundane tattoo with no magic in it at all. They
travel in **caravans** and have **no central power structure** — no king, no capital, just bands
and the road.

### The Birdfolk

**Avian** people in an enormous variety of **shapes**, their **plumage** a riot of colors and
styles. They are **good-natured and intensely social** — but **not gossips.** What they love is
**genuine stories**: hearing them, and carrying them on. Not Brall's tall tales, not Vint's
gossip — *true* stories, told for their own sake. They favor **high places** (cliffs, roosts),
though some keep **houses in the cities.** They have **their own gods.**

### The Merfolk

An ocean people, **xenophobic**, who consider themselves **superior to land-folk.** They **do
trade** — but rarely warmly, and the precise root of their disdain is **unclear** even to those
who deal with them. They keep **their own gods**, and many do not speak Common. They are the
sea's standing reminder that not every wonder in this world wants the player's friendship.

### Rentacosta — the free city of the coast

A **coastal free city** built on **sailing and seaborne commerce**, trading up and down the
coasts. Its people are famously **worldly** and **almost all multilingual.** Rentacosta is a
**relatively relaxed** place — though a **hard** life, since the long voyages leave families
behind for seasons at a stretch. They **trade with the merfolk more than anyone else dares**,
and they are **widely tolerated and respected**: they're useful to everyone, and harming them
would strike most people as plainly **criminal**. A natural neutral ground, a place that speaks
to everyone.

## The Texture of Everyday Life

**Money and tongue.** A **common coin** circulates across most of the world (notably **less** so
in whichever realm is the current rival). **Common** is the shared language of the imperial
realms; the holdouts where many people still **wouldn't speak Common** are **Ontria, Gontark, and
the merfolk.**

**The seams of empire.** There are **imperial roads** and **post offices** stitching the realms
together, and — the part that makes the Empire hard to simply hate — **people genuinely enjoy the
peace.** Roads are safe, trade flows, the charter really has prevented magical catastrophes. The
marble is comfortable. That comfort is the antagonist.

**Cross-realm feeling is emergent, not canon.** What a Brallman thinks of a Stalnazan, or a
Ryolani of a Vintan, is left to **individual characters** — it varies person to person, and the
emergent-world/NPC systems should generate it rather than reading from a fixed stereotype table.
The realms have strong characters; their *opinions of each other* are improvised at the table.

**Wild magic in the popular imagination** is deliberately **ambivalent** — never a simple
boogeyman:

- Many people are **nostalgic** for the old stories of their ancestral traditions, and so carry
  real **sympathy** for sorcerers — for the freedom wild magic represents. A good number have
  **secretly wanted to try it**, and some quietly **have** (remember: nearly everyone has a
  little capacity).
- And yet the **fear is legitimate**, not mere propaganda: beyond the Shadow Purge, wild magic
  is **inherently unpredictable** and can serve **great evil as easily as great good.** A
  reasonable person can love the freedom and dread the danger at once.

That doubled feeling — longing *and* fear, in the same ordinary heart — is the social weather the
player moves through. To most folk, a wild mage is not a monster and not a hero. They're a
**possibility**, equal parts thrilling and terrifying, that the marble has promised to keep
safely filed away.

## Naming & Voice (for the content systems)

Per the tone bible's naming split: **wild/folk** things take **earthy English compounds**
(bone-singer, horse-singing, the Glasswild, the Saltmarket); the **Empire** takes **cold
Latinate officialese** (the Censorate, Thaumic Containment, Provincial Edict). Established proper
nouns from this document, safe to use verbatim:

- **Empire:** the Grand Empire of Vigovia · Vigovia (the heartland) · Vigo the Lawgiver · the
  Censorate · the Shadow Purge · charter magic / charter mages
- **Old kingdoms:** Stalnaz (queen, the founding holiday) · Brall (Bone Jarls, holds, scrimshaw)
  · Ryolan (the king, chariot teams) · Vint (the republic, woven charms)
- **Client & small realms:** the Independent Kingdom of Threen · Monteary · Ontria · Gontark ·
  the Parn · Rentacosta
- **Peoples:** birdfolk · merfolk · goatfolk (Gontark)

Quick region-voice handles for prompt injection (one line each — expand later):

- **Vigovia/imperial:** clipped, courteous, passive-voice, file-numbered. Beards and ledgers.
- **Stalnaz:** elegant, allusive, musical; speaks in resonance, light, and patient refinement.
- **Brall:** warm, boastful-about-others, escalating; ale-loud, story-drunk.
- **Ryolan:** formal, honor-weighted, blunt; oaths and first-blood.
- **Vint:** quick, sly, rumor-threaded; everything is who-said-what.
- **Threen:** literary, mannered, performative-polite over a swallowed truth.
- **The Parn:** sung, rhythmic, road-worn; ink and music.
- **Birdfolk:** bright, generous, story-hungry; sincere not sly.
- **Merfolk:** cold, superior, sparing.
- **Rentacosta:** code-switching, easygoing, salt-worn.
